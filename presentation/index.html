Continual learning for deployed agents

Agents that keep learning after they ship.

Today's agents are frozen the moment they deploy. We built Continuum — a test-time asynchronous-RL loop that lets a deployed agent improve from its own experience — and SDPO, a staleness-aware update that makes that loop stable.

Chandra Suda  ·  Aaditya Nalawade Stanford · Symbolic Systems
↓ Continuum & SDPO
01 — The problem

A deployed agent is a snapshot of a world that won't hold still.

The instant an agent ships, its weights stop changing. The world it works in does not — a new library version, an unseen product, a codebase that changed last night.

The expensive part of knowledge work demands adaptation, and in-context tricks don't compound: nothing the agent learns today survives to tomorrow. Continual learning is the missing piece — let the agent keep improving from its own deployment experience.

Why this matters

The defensible products in knowledge work aren't the ones that answer a static benchmark — they're the ones that get sharper on your codebase, your customers, the longer you use them.

Why now

Agentic products already emit exactly what RL needs — rollouts plus outcomes — as a byproduct of doing their job. The training data is free; we just have to close the loop.
02 — What we built

Continuum: test-time asynchronous RL.

An agent — coding agent, support bot, ops agent — runs in a sandbox and stores every rollout with its outcome. We pool those rollouts across many instances and run an always-on RL trainer that pushes improved weights back to the fleet.

rollout + reward, streaming updated weights, broadcast reward signal

Self-graded rollouts

In verifiable domains (code, kernels, math) the agent writes its own unit tests and grades itself at test time. The reward needs no human in the loop.

Human-graded rollouts

In unverifiable domains (support, writing) we collect user feedback or label a batch before training. Same buffer, different reward source.

Modal sandboxes

The fleet and the trainer run on Modal sandboxes — cheap parallel rollout collection, decoupled from a continuously-updating learner.

The catch: "always-on" means off-policy. By the time the trainer sees a rollout, the policy has already moved on. That gap — staleness — is what breaks naive async RL, and it's what SDPO is for.
03 — The staleness problem & SDPO

Stale rollouts make updates explode. We clip where it matters.

Off-policy, the importance ratio rt = πnew/πold drifts from 1. Its variance is heavy-tailed: a few tokens dominate the gradient and training collapses. Every fix trades a little bias for a lot less variance.

baseline A

KL penalty

Pull πnew toward πold globally. Blunt — it pays a price even for drift that was perfectly safe.

baseline B

Ratio clipping (PPO)

Clip rt to a flat [1−ε, 1+ε]. The "Trajectory" recipe: one knob, conservative everywhere.

baseline C

Advantage clipping

Bound the advantage instead of the ratio. Stabilizes magnitude but ignores which tokens are actually risky.

ours
SDPO

Entropy-scaled clip

A per-token threshold that tightens where the teacher is confident and loosens where it isn't. Conservative where it matters, permissive where it doesn't.

εt = ε0 · ( Ht Hmean )α
then clip rt to [ 1 − εt ,   1 + εt ]

Ht is the teacher's entropy at token t; Hmean is a running EMA of teacher entropy across recent batches. Two knobs instead of one — but both are derivable: ε0 is the average tolerance (match Trajectory's 0.2), and α falls out of a variance-budget calculation — set it so the expected variance contribution is uniform across the entropy distribution.

Why it beats a flat clip. Trajectory prevents collapse by being conservative everywhere. SDPO prevents collapse by being conservative only at peaked-teacher tokens — the ones where a stale update actually does damage — and staying permissive elsewhere, so we keep the off-policy data we can safely use.

Two measurable wins

1. Lower seed-to-seed variance at any staleness K — directly attacking Trajectory's pain point, the 0.10–0.45 eval band at identical hyperparameters.

2. Graceful accuracy preservation at higher K than a flat clip can handle.

05 — A fully-verifiable testbed: kernel synthesis

To stress the loop, we manufactured RL environments for GPU-kernel engineering.

Kernels are the perfect petri dish for the verifiable arm of Continuum: the reward is dense, cheap, and automatic — numerical correctness times speedup. So we built kernel-synth, a factory that turns real GitHub repos into kernel-writing RL tasks.

06 — The bet

Agents that get better the more they're used.

The data exhaust of agentic products — rollouts plus outcomes — becomes the training signal. Asynchronous RL turns it into compounding improvement, and SDPO keeps that loop from collapsing under its own staleness: the difference between a tool that plateaus and one that climbs.

What we shipped

The Continuum loop on Modal, the SDPO update, the LiveCodeBench study, and the kernel-synth verifiable testbed.

What's next

Derive α from the variance budget in closed form; scale rollout collection; bring the unverifiable (support) arm online with human feedback.
